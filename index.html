Postpartum Thrombophlebitis

DVT · Superficial · Pelvic Thrombophlebitis | Adele Pillitteri, 7e

Definition & Epidemiology

Thrombophlebitis – inflammation of a vein associated with thrombus (clot) formation. In the puerperium, pregnancy‑induced hypercoagulability dramatically increases risk. Includes deep vein thrombosis (DVT), superficial vein thrombosis (SVT), and septic pelvic thrombophlebitis.

📊 Incidence
Pregnancy increases DVT risk 5- to 10‑fold. Incidence of postpartum DVT: 0.5–1 per 1000 deliveries. Cesarean section doubles/triples risk. Most DVTs occur in the left leg.
⏰ Timing
Highest risk in the first 6 weeks postpartum, peak at 1‑3 weeks after delivery. Antepartum risk is also elevated but greatest postnatally.
Key concept: Virchow's triad – venous stasis, endothelial injury, hypercoagulability – all present in pregnancy/puerperium.

Risk Factors & Etiology

⚠️ Major Risk Factors
Cesarean section (especially emergent), obesity, age >35, multiparity, prolonged bed rest/immobility, preeclampsia, postpartum hemorrhage, infection, dehydration, varicose veins, smoking, prior thrombosis, thrombophilia (Factor V Leiden, prothrombin mutation, protein C/S deficiency).
🧬 Virchow’s Triad in Pregnancy
Hypercoagulability (increased fibrinogen, Factors VII‑X, decreased protein S), venous stasis (enlarged uterus compresses iliac veins, left common iliac vein), endothelial injury (vaginal/cesarean delivery).
📊 Laterality: Left leg DVTs are 2‑3 times more common due to compression of left common iliac vein by right iliac artery (May‑Thurner anatomy).

Types: Superficial vs Deep vs Pelvic

🔹 Superficial Vein Thrombosis (SVT)
Affects saphenous or superficial leg veins; usually due to varicose veins or IV trauma. Palpable, tender cord, erythema. Low risk of PE, but can extend to deep system.
🔸 Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)
Most common in iliofemoral or popliteal veins. Unilateral leg swelling, pain, warmth, Homan’s sign (often unreliable). High risk of pulmonary embolism (PE).
🩸 Septic Pelvic Thrombophlebitis
Rare complication of postpartum endometritis. Persistent fever despite antibiotics, unilateral lower quadrant pain, palpable tender cord (ovarian vein). Treatment: anticoagulation + antibiotics.
Phlegmasia cerulea dolens: Massive iliofemoral DVT causing severe edema, cyanosis, compartment syndrome – limb‑threatening emergency.

Clinical Manifestations & Diagnosis

  • DVT symptoms: Unilateral leg pain, swelling (edema), warmth, erythema, tenderness over deep veins, positive Homan’s sign (pain on dorsiflexion) – unreliable.
  • Well’s score: Clinical prediction rule for DVT probability.
  • Diagnostic tests: Compression ultrasound (Doppler) is first‑line (high sensitivity for proximal DVT). D‑dimer (less specific postpartum due to physiologic elevation). Venography for equivocal cases.
  • Pelvic thrombophlebitis: Fever >38°C for >72h despite antibiotics, unilateral lower quadrant pain, CT/MRI showing thrombosed ovarian vein.
  • Superficial thrombophlebitis: Palpable tender “cord”, localized redness, no significant edema.
🚨 Caution: Homan’s sign is neither sensitive nor specific; do NOT forcibly dorsiflex if DVT suspected (may dislodge clot).

Medical & Nursing Management

  • Anticoagulation (acute DVT/PE): Low‑molecular‑weight heparin (LMWH) – enoxaparin 1 mg/kg BID or once daily; unfractionated heparin IV (monitor aPTT). Warfarin (Coumadin) is avoided in pregnancy but may be used postpartum (safe for breastfeeding); overlapping with heparin until INR 2‑3.
  • Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs): Rivaroxaban, apixaban – not recommended during pregnancy but may be used postpartum (limited data).
  • Supportive care: Bed rest with leg elevation, warm compresses (for superficial), analgesics, compression stockings (after acute phase).
  • Nursing interventions: Monitor for bleeding (gums, lochia, injection sites). Instruct patient not to massage leg. Maintain IV access, monitor vitals, and assess for signs of PE (sudden dyspnea, chest pain, hemoptysis, tachycardia).
  • Septic pelvic thrombophlebitis: Antibiotics + heparin (therapeutic anticoagulation).
  • Superficial thrombophlebitis: NSAIDs, warm compresses, compression stockings; anticoagulation rarely needed unless extension.
Breastfeeding: LMWH, warfarin, and DOACs are considered compatible (warfarin not secreted into milk).

Complications & Prevention

⚠️ Complications
Pulmonary embolism (PE) – most feared; signs: sudden dyspnea, chest pain, hemoptysis, tachycardia, hypotension. Post‑thrombotic syndrome (chronic leg swelling, pain, skin changes, ulcers). Recurrent DVT. Phlegmasia cerulea dolens (limb ischemia).
🛡️ Prevention (Pharmacologic)
Routine thromboprophylaxis for high‑risk women (cesarean + additional risk factors). LMWH (enoxaparin 40 mg daily) starting 6‑12 hours after delivery. Mechanical prophylaxis (sequential compression devices, elastic stockings) for all C‑sections.
🚶 Non‑pharmacologic prevention
Early ambulation, hydration, leg exercises, avoid prolonged bed rest, avoid constrictive clothing, stop smoking.
🔍 Risk assessment
Use Royal College of Obstetricians (RCOG) thromboprophylaxis risk assessment tool.
🚨 PE emergency: Call code, high‑flow oxygen, IV access, prepare for thrombolysis or embolectomy if hemodynamically unstable.

Mnemonics & Summary

📌 Virchow’s Triad – “S.E.I.”
Stasis, Endothelial injury, Hypercoagulability.
🩸 DVT Signs “S.W.A.P.”
Swelling, Warmth, Area tenderness, Pain (unilateral).
⚠️ PE Symptoms “C.T.H.U.M.P.”
Chest pain, Tachypnea, Hemoptysis, Uneasy (anxiety), Murmur (tachycardia), Pulmonary hypertension signs.
💊 Anticoagulation in pregnancy/lactation
LMWH is safe and first‑line; warfarin safe postpartum (not in pregnancy); avoid DOACs in pregnancy but may use postpartum.
📋 Risk factors “C.O.A.L.S.”
Cesarean section, Obesity, Age >35, Large baby/ multiparity, Smoking/prior thrombosis.
🛌 Prevention “M.E.D.S.”
Mobilize early, Exercise legs, Drugs (LMWH), Sequential compression devices.
